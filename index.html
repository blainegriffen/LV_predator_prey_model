Lotka-Volterra Predator-Prey Simulator

Developed by Blaine Griffen

Classic Lotka-Volterra model with exponential prey growth (no carrying capacity). Adjust prey growth (r), attack rate (a), conversion efficiency (b), and predator mortality (m) to see predator–prey cycles, extinctions, and other dynamics in both time series and phase-plane views.

How to use this simulator

  • Start with Undamped cycles (classic LV) to see long-lived oscillations in both time series and phase plane.
  • Compare presets to see how changing a, b, or m makes predators go extinct or lets prey escape.
  • Use the phase plane to emphasize that classic LV trajectories move around closed orbits defined by the intersection of the isoclines.
  • Experiment with different initial conditions N(0) and P(0) to see how starting densities choose which orbit the system follows.

N = prey, P = predators. Prey grow exponentially with rate r. Predators remove prey at rate aNP, convert a fraction b of that prey into new predators, and die at rate mP.

Phase plane: prey vs predators

Each point shows the state (N,P) at a moment in time. The trajectory arrowheads show the direction of motion through time. Dashed lines are the zero-growth isoclines: P=r/a for prey and N=m/(ba) for predators.

Final prey N(tmax): 10.59, Final predators P(tmax): 10.74

Outcome: Predators and prey both persist: the system follows a closed or nearly closed orbit rather than settling to a fixed point.

How to interpret this run:

Both prey and predators remain at non-zero densities, and the system cycles indefinitely rather than converging to a fixed point. This is characteristic of the classic Lotka-Volterra model with exponential prey growth. Because b·a·N is small relative to m, each predator has a hard time replacing itself, which pushes the system toward predator decline.
